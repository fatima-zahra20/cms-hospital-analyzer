Establishing the paradox

Does higher spending produce better clinical outcomes?

To answer this, a composite outcome score was built from 23 CMS benchmark signals across 5 clinical dimensions — mortality, patient safety, readmissions, infections, and patient experience. Each signal flags whether a hospital performs better (+1), average (0), or worse (−1) than the national rate set by CMS. The total score ranges from −23 to +23, where 0 represents perfectly average performance — not failure.

Every hospital was then classified by whether it spends above or below the national Medicare average and whether its outcome score is positive or negative. The result: a correlation of just 0.24 between spending and outcomes — meaning the vast majority of what determines whether a hospital performs well has nothing to do with how much money it spends.

A correlation of 0.24 means that 76% of clinical outcome variation is explained by something other than money. Spending is a weak predictor at best. This is the foundation of the paradox this analysis sets out to explain.

The entire analysis focuses on the paradox zone — the 99 hospitals occupying the worst possible combination. They spend above average while delivering below-average clinical outcomes across the majority of the 23 CMS benchmarks. These are not edge cases. They are a measurable, identifiable group whose failures have a financial fingerprint and a clinical cost.

Note on the outcome score: A score of 0 is not a bad hospital — it means performing exactly at the national average. A hospital is only flagged as a paradox case if its net signals are negative, meaning more clinical dimensions are worse than average than are better. CMS risk-adjusts its benchmark comparisons for patient population, so the score accounts for expected performance given the type of patients a hospital treats.

Profiling the paradox hospitals

Who are these 99 hospitals?

Before asking why they underperform, the first question is who they are. Three dimensions were examined: state concentration, ownership type, and hospital type. The goal was to find a structural pattern — if the 99 share common characteristics, that points to a systemic explanation rather than random failure.

A common counter-argument at this stage is the case mix hypothesis — the idea that these hospitals spend more and perform worse because they treat more complex, sicker patients. This analysis tests that assumption at every step.

Process failures

Where is the money actually going?

Three process dimensions were measured and compared between the 99 paradox hospitals and all other hospitals in the dataset: infection rates (HAI/SIR), 30-day readmissions, and excess acute care days (EDAC). EDAC measures days spent in acute care beyond what is clinically expected — and CMS already risk-adjusts this measure for patient severity, which means the extended stays cannot be explained by sicker patients.

Patients are not outraged — they rate their doctors and nurses reasonably well. But every single dimension scores lower at paradox hospitals. No category where they match or beat their peers. The most telling gap is discharge information at −3.52. Patients feel less prepared leaving these hospitals — and this directly mirrors the clinical finding from Layer 3: poor discharge planning is a measurable failure both in the data and in the patient's lived experience of it.

The star rating gap of 0.85 is significant on a 1–5 scale. Paradox hospitals average 2.42 stars — closer to 2 than 3. Patients are quietly signaling that something is not right, even when they cannot name exactly what. The data names it for them.

"The hospitals that spend the most on dysfunction are the hospitals that invest the least in process. The spending is not the cause of poor outcomes — it is the financial cost of them."

The answer

Spending does not drive outcomes. Process quality does.

The 99 paradox hospitals spend $3,485 more per patient not because they are investing in better care — but because their internal process failures generate cascading costs that no amount of money can retroactively fix. The correlation between spending and outcomes is 0.24. Seventy-six percent of what determines whether a hospital performs well has nothing to do with its budget.

These hospitals are not treating sicker patients. They are all Acute Care facilities, spread across all ownership types, concentrated in smaller states that are not known for high-complexity medical hubs. The case mix counter-argument fails at every layer of this analysis.

The problem is operational. And it is a policy problem hiding in plain sight inside the CMS data — data that was always there to be read.

Limitations and future work: This analysis cannot confirm patient volume, staffing levels, or internal discharge planning records — data not available in the CMS public dataset. The why behind the extended stays points to process failures, but a complete causal explanation would require hospital-level operational data. Future layers of this project will investigate geographic clustering, timeliness of care measures, and whether CMS penalty exposure correlates with paradox hospital status.
